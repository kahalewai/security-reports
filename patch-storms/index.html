The reframe · Three parallel stories

One event. Three layers. Each stands alone.

Most of the coverage has picked a single thread and run with it. The honest account requires holding three at once. Tap any card to read the full analysis.

This is the signal, and the story with the longest industry implications. Before February 2026, finding a high-severity browser vulnerability was bottlenecked on elite human researcher time: months of code reading per bug, a global talent pool of maybe a few hundred people capable of doing this work on a target like Firefox, and market prices reflecting that scarcity. Pwn2Own paid tens of thousands of dollars per working Firefox exploit. Brokers on the offensive side paid six figures. That pricing existed because supply was constrained.

In February 2026, a single Claude Opus 4.6 scan of roughly 6,000 C++ files in Firefox produced 112 bug reports, 22 of which shipped as CVEs in Firefox 148 — 14 rated high severity. That output from one scan exceeds what most elite human researchers produce in a year of focused work on the same target. The scan takes hours of compute, not months of headcount.

The unit cost of finding a well-understood vulnerability class — memory safety, JIT miscompilation, use-after-free — dropped by what appears to be more than an order of magnitude. Both defenders and attackers now have access to tools with that capability.

The implication is not that AI found novel classes of bugs. Mozilla's own blog explicitly states these were all bugs an elite human could have found. The implication is that the asymmetric advantage attackers have historically enjoyed — needing to find only one bug while defenders must find them all — is eroding in the direction of whoever deploys scanning capability at scale first.

Every security team with a memory-unsafe codebase should be modeling what their attack surface looks like when the cost of a vulnerability scan drops 10–100x. Because the people targeting them are modeling it right now.

This is what the chart actually shows, and it is defensible without any appeal to the 271 figure. Firefox's eleven pre-AI releases — 137 through 147, April 2025 through January 2026 — averaged 12.9 CVEs per release with a range of 8 to 19. Normal variance. The three AI-assisted releases (148, 149, 150) averaged 43 CVEs per release. That is a 3.3× step function, not a gradual ramp, and the timing aligns exactly with the public disclosure of the Anthropic partnership.

Firefox 148 alone shipped more CVEs than any other release in the 14-release window, with 22 of those 51 (43 percent) carrying the explicit using Claude from Anthropic attribution in the advisory. Before February 2026, that attribution line did not exist in any Firefox advisory. That is your before-and-after moment, and it is fully documented.

What gets found has to get patched. Which means the defender's patch pipeline becomes the new bottleneck. Mozilla had to mobilize additional engineering capacity to triage, fix, and ship 51 CVEs in a four-week release window. Most vendors cannot do that.

For enterprise security teams, this means AI-assisted discovery — when it arrives at your dependencies — will arrive as patch storms rather than zero-days. Your vulnerability management program's SLA assumptions (time to test, time to deploy, maintenance window capacity) were built for the 13-CVE world, not the 43-CVE world.

That is a concrete operational posture question every CISO should be asking this quarter: does our patch pipeline scale to a 3x volume increase on our most critical internet-exposed software, without breaking our change management process? If the honest answer is no, that is where the work is.

Two documents went out from Mozilla on April 21, 2026, describing the same Firefox 150 release. The blog post said 271 vulnerabilities were found and fixed. The formal security advisory — MFSA 2026-30 — lists 41 CVEs, of which 3 are credited to Claude. Both documents are from the same vendor on the same day. They disagree by a factor of roughly 90.

The memory-safety roll-up CVEs, which one might assume absorb the missing 268 defects, explicitly credit Mozilla's internal Fuzzing Team with no mention of Anthropic. Independent analysts flagged the discrepancy within 24 hours. The reconciliation may come in Anthropic's July 2026 Project Glasswing transparency report, or it may not.

The same AI capability that compresses the cost of finding vulnerabilities also compresses the cost of generating plausible-sounding marketing claims about security posture — from every vendor, in every category.

Security teams in 2026 need a verification reflex: see a headline number, find the primary source, check whether the primary source supports the headline, note the delta, publish your assessment with the delta visible. This is not a criticism of Mozilla specifically. Mozilla is unusual in that they publish the primary source at all, which is why the discrepancy is auditable. Many vendors do not.

Read the advisory, not the press release. Make it a habit.

There is a darker parallel worth naming explicitly: if defender-side vendors are already inflating discovery numbers for positive framing, attacker-side actors have every incentive to deflate or hide the same capability. The public signal you see is likely the noisy version of both sides. Plan accordingly.

The playbook · Before the next storm

Four moves before the next 40-CVE release lands.

No new tooling required. Four decisions to make now, not during the next cycle.

01 Operations

Rebuild the SLA math for the 43-CVE world.

Your vulnerability management program was designed around a 13-CVE baseline. Audit test-and-deploy throughput, maintenance-window capacity, and change-approval bandwidth against the new cadence. Where the math no longer closes, either add capacity or formally revise SLAs — don't let them drift silently into non-compliance.

02 Agents

Point agents at your own code. This week.

The same AI capability generating the storm is equally available to defenders. Start this week by pointing an agent at a single high-exposure module for security review. Build toward LLM-driven review inside your CI/CD pipeline so all code — human or AI-generated — passes a scan before merge. Tools exist today, commercial (Claude Code Security, Codex Security) and open source (OpenAnt, raptor). The economic compression cuts both ways — capture it.

03 Verification

Make primary-source verification a standing workflow.

When a vendor publishes a security headline, your team should already know the drill: pull the advisory, note the delta against the headline, circulate an internal assessment. Don't let a blog post set patching priority. Build the reflex before the next disclosure cycle, not during it.

04 Supply chain

Watch the upstream that looks most like Mozilla.

Chromium, major OS kernels, runtime language teams, large cloud providers — any vendor with both the codebase scale and the security-team maturity to replicate Mozilla's approach is a leading indicator. Track their advisory cadence now. The first storm is rarely the only storm.
