Layer 00 · Entry Consent

Vendor selection & OAuth consent

A Vercel employee authorized Context.ai against their corporate Google Workspace. Likely no central security review. The attack surface was created at this moment — quietly, legitimately, years before the compromise.

› Expand full analysis
What happened

An employee granted OAuth scopes to a third-party AI agent platform — almost certainly including gmail.readonly and drive.readonly. Given industry baselines, the grant almost certainly bypassed central security review; most Workspace tenants still allow users to authorize arbitrary OAuth apps by default.

The moment the grant existed, Context.ai held a long-lived OAuth refresh token into the employee's Workspace account. Refresh tokens are silent (no user interaction required to mint access tokens), persistent (they survive password changes unless explicitly revoked), and MFA-bypassing (they don't re-prompt for the second factor).

Why it enabled the next layer
The moment Context.ai was compromised upstream, that refresh token became functionally equivalent to the employee's Workspace credentials. No attack on Google was required — the credential was delegated legitimately, and the grant had no expiry.
What could have prevented it
  • Admin-level restriction on third-party OAuth app authorization in Workspace — shift default from "any user can authorize" to "admin must approve sensitive scopes"
  • Mandatory vendor security review for any SaaS requesting Gmail, Drive, Calendar, or Admin SDK scopes
  • Scope minimization policy — push vendors toward resource-specific grants (single mailbox, single folder) over tenant-wide .readonly
  • Periodic audit with automatic expiry for unused OAuth grants (default 90-day idle revocation)
  • Heightened scrutiny on AI tool authorizations — they typically request broader scopes than their stated function requires
Layer 01 · Upstream Ingress unknown

Context.ai compromise

Context.ai was breached — method not publicly disclosed, no post-mortem published. The attacker obtained OAuth refresh tokens for every tenant that had authorized Context.ai, including the Vercel employee's Workspace.

› Expand full analysis
What happened

Context.ai — an enterprise AI agent platform whose product deploys autonomous agents inside customer systems (Salesforce, Slack, Google Workspace, billing systems) — was compromised. The attacker obtained control of, or extracted tokens from, Context.ai's Workspace OAuth application. The ingress method has not been publicly disclosed.

A relevant wrinkle: the domain context.ai was acquired by a different company (formerly known as context.inc) after the original Context.ai was acqui-hired by OpenAI in April 2025. Users' OAuth consents survived the ownership change. The company behind the domain today is not necessarily the company users decided to trust.

Plausible ingress vectors (ranked by threat-landscape priors)
  • Conventional SaaS compromise — credential theft via infostealer or phishing, cloud-infra misconfiguration, compromised CI/CD, or vulnerability in the token-storage layer. This is how Salesloft Drift was owned in August 2025.
  • AI-agent-native compromise — indirect prompt injection via untrusted content the agents processed, instructing an agent to exfiltrate its own OAuth tokens. Confused-deputy attacks native to the agent era; already documented in MCP breach incidents (GitHub MCP, mcp-remote CVE-2025-6514, Anthropic Filesystem MCP).
  • Credential storage weakness — industry anecdote suggests agent platforms not uncommonly store tokens in plaintext config files, .md files, or loosely-secured databases rather than HSMs or dedicated token vaults.
Why it enabled the next layer
With valid refresh tokens, the attacker could mint fresh access tokens on demand for every tenant that had authorized Context.ai. This bypassed all MFA, password, and most anomaly-detection controls because the requests came through a legitimately-consented OAuth flow.
What could have prevented it
  • Context.ai side: HSM-backed token storage, per-tenant encryption, token custody isolated from general application infrastructure
  • Short-lived refresh tokens with proof-of-possession binding (DPoP or mTLS)
  • Prompt-injection hardening — tool-use authorization gates for any agent action touching credential material
  • Customer side: don't grant broad scopes to begin with; treat every authorized vendor as a pre-breached identity
Layer 02 · Pivot Identity takeover

OAuth token abuse & Workspace account takeover

Using the stolen tokens, the attacker entered the employee's Google Workspace. Mailbox access was sufficient to execute full identity takeover through standard password-reset tradecraft.

› Expand full analysis
What happened
The attacker accessed the Vercel employee's Google Workspace using the stolen Context.ai OAuth refresh tokens. At minimum, this gave mailbox read access. From there, standard identity-takeover tradecraft applies: trigger password reset on ancillary accounts, intercept magic links, reset MFA, create app-specific passwords, export Drive content, read Calendar for organizational mapping.
Why it enabled the next layer
Full control of the Google Workspace account is full control of the federated identity. Because Vercel federates workforce SSO through Google, the Google account is the Vercel account. No additional authentication boundary exists between them.
What could have prevented it
  • Phishing-resistant MFA (hardware keys, passkeys) on the Workspace IdP — no SMS or TOTP fallback
  • Google Advanced Protection Program enrollment for production-access roles
  • Session-level monitoring on OAuth access-token refresh events, not just login events
  • Device-bound sessions (Chrome DBSC or equivalent) that invalidate on unfamiliar device
  • Short refresh token lifetimes with automatic revocation on anomaly signals (new IP, ASN, UA)
Layer 03 · Federation SSO inheritance

SSO pivot into Vercel

The attacker signed into Vercel via SSO. Not an exploit — the designed authentication path, invoked by an attacker who had become the legitimate principal.

› Expand full analysis
What happened
With control of the Google Workspace account, the attacker signed into Vercel via SSO federation. This was not an exploit — it was the designed authentication path. Per CEO Guillermo Rauch, the attacker then executed "a series of maneuvers that escalated" access — unspecified, but consistent with enumerating projects, listing environments, pulling integration configurations, and harvesting additional credentials discoverable from the initial authenticated position.
Why it enabled the next layer
The attacker was now an authenticated Vercel user with the employee's full dashboard and API permissions. A single SSO session was sufficient to read every environment variable in every project the employee had access to — no step-up authentication required.
What could have prevented it
  • Step-up authentication for sensitive actions inside Vercel — reading env vars, modifying production deployments, managing integrations
  • Device trust / managed-device posture enforcement for SSO into production SaaS
  • Role segmentation — least-privilege scoping of internal access to shrink blast radius
  • Anomaly detection on first-seen-IP / first-seen-device per user, with forced re-authentication via out-of-band channel (not email — which the attacker controls)
Layer 04 · Harvest Default-off

Environment variable enumeration

The attacker read every variable not flagged "sensitive." Encryption at rest was irrelevant — the attacker held an authenticated session. What mattered was read-back, and that was not protected by default.

› Expand full analysis
What happened

The attacker enumerated and read environment variables not flagged as sensitive. Per Rauch's April 20 update, all Vercel environment variables are encrypted at rest — the sensitive flag does not control encryption; it controls read-back. Non-sensitive variables can be decrypted and returned to authenticated principals through the UI and API; sensitive variables cannot be retrieved even by Vercel's own tooling once set.

The attacker had a legitimate authenticated session. Encryption-at-rest was irrelevant. What mattered was read-back — and that was not protected by default.

Why it enabled the next layer
Non-sensitive variables commonly contain exactly the secrets they should not: API keys, database credentials, signing keys, OAuth client secrets, third-party tokens. Per Vercel's bulletin and community reporting, tokens for Linear and GitHub integrations appear to have been among those exposed, enabling further lateral movement.
The architectural finding

Secure-by-default is the only default that scales. The sensitive flag worked exactly as designed — it was also opt-in. Developers who knew and remembered were protected; everyone else was not.

This is the dominant failure mode of every opt-in security control: encrypted S3 buckets (historically opt-in until AWS flipped the default), private GitHub repos, Kubernetes RBAC defaults. Any control labeled "turn this on for better security" is an incident report waiting to be written.

What could have prevented it
  • Secure-by-default: the sensitive flag should default to ON, with opt-out requiring explicit justification
  • Elimination of long-lived secrets entirely — workload identity federation (OIDC to AWS/GCP/Azure/Vault) replaces static API keys with short-lived credentials
  • Automatic secret scanning on write — any string matching a known secret format auto-flagged as sensitive
  • Read-access audit logging with anomaly detection on bulk enumeration patterns
Layer 05 · Lateral Cross-SaaS

Lateral movement: Linear, GitHub, npm

Secrets harvested from env vars unlocked adjacent SaaS platforms. Not lateral movement across a network — across the SaaS mesh. Traditional segmentation provides zero defense.

› Expand full analysis
What happened
Per community reporting (not yet confirmed by Vercel), the attacker used secrets and integration tokens stored in Vercel to pivot into Linear and GitHub. The attacker-claimed haul on BreachForums includes source code, internal deployment access, NPM tokens, and GitHub tokens — offered for $2M. Vercel has explicitly confirmed that Next.js, Turbopack, and its open-source projects remain safe after a supply-chain audit — the most consequential defensive outcome of the incident, since a poisoned Next.js publish would have been an FBI-advisory-level event.
The architectural shift
Tokens harvested from one SaaS unlock access to adjacent platforms in the same development pipeline. This is lateral movement across the SaaS mesh, not across a corporate network. Firewalls, network segmentation, and NIDS provide zero defense — nothing crossed a network boundary worth monitoring. The investment most orgs made in perimeter security does not apply to the attack paths that actually matter now.
What could have prevented it
  • Integration tokens scoped per-purpose, per-repository, per-environment — never org-wide
  • Automated supply-chain audits independent of the primary platform — anomalous publish detection in near-real-time
  • Write operations on critical publish pipelines gated by out-of-band approval (second human, hardware key, signed release artifact)
  • Monitoring npm and GitHub for anomalous publishes, token usage from new locations, new maintainer additions across org-critical packages
The fourth plane

OAuth is a credential.
We do not govern it like one.

Enterprises conceptualize identity as three planes: passwords, MFA, SSO. OAuth consent is a fourth — and it has fundamentally different properties than the other three.

Architectural truth №6
What a veteran architect says

Seven architectural truths

Reframings — not recommendations. The test for each: does it make the reader reconsider an assumption they'd been operating on? Click any statement to expand the reasoning.

Truth 01 ›

OAuth consent is a credential issuance event. Govern it like one.

Most organizations have rigorous governance for passwords, SSH keys, API keys, and certificates. They treat OAuth authorization like app installation. An OAuth grant has the security-relevant properties of a password: it bypasses MFA on use, survives password rotation, rarely appears in any credential inventory, and is almost never monitored. Every grant your company has ever approved is a standing, silent, privileged identity you are still responsible for.
Truth 02 ›

Your security ceiling is set by the weakest vendor you authorized, not the strongest control you deployed.

The best lock on the front door is irrelevant if the building has two hundred side doors, each held open by a third party whose security program you have never audited. Vendor risk management is not a compliance exercise — it is the perimeter. The next major incident in your organization will almost certainly enter through a SaaS you granted access to three years ago and then stopped thinking about.
Truth 03 ›

Any security control that requires a human to remember to enable it has already failed.

Every opt-in security feature with a meaningful failure population proves this: encrypted S3 buckets, private GitHub repos, sensitive environment variables, MFA enforcement, secret scanning. The pattern is universal — the default is what gets used at scale. Secure-by-default, with opt-out friction and documented justification, is the only design that survives production.
Truth 04 ›

Trust is a decision. OAuth consent is a persistent artifact. They do not expire together.

When a user authorizes an application, they consent to an entity at a point in time. When the entity changes — through acquisition, domain transfer, product pivot, or wind-down — the grant remains. OAuth 2.0 has no mechanism for forced re-consent when the underlying entity changes hands. Your vendor inventory must track ownership history, not just current state. The Context.ai case made this concrete: the domain changed owners, the consents did not.
Truth 05 ›

AI agent platforms are token aggregators by design, not by accident.

The business model of an enterprise AI agent platform is holding broad-scope, long-lived credentials across many systems for many tenants. A single compromise is equivalent to a coordinated breach of every connected SaaS for every customer. This is not a flaw in their implementation — it is intrinsic to the product category. Agent platforms must therefore be evaluated against a stricter vendor-security bar than other SaaS, not a laxer one.
Truth 06 ›

Attackers do not move through networks anymore. They move through identity graphs.

The Context.ai → Workspace → Vercel → Linear → GitHub pivot crossed zero network boundaries. Every lateral movement was legitimately-federated authentication using valid credentials at every hop. Firewalls, network segmentation, and NIDS provided zero defense. Identity-graph mapping, OAuth-plane monitoring, and SSO anomaly detection are the new NIDS — and most organizations' maturity here is roughly where network security was in 2005.
Truth 07 ›

Post-compromise tempo is compressing from weeks to hours. Response built for the old tempo will not detect the new one.

The traditional SOC model — collect logs, investigate at human pace, escalate when certain — was built for an adversary constrained by human effort. AI-accelerated attackers are not. The window between initial foothold and complete objective may now be measured in hours. A response architecture that depends on an analyst triaging an alert the next morning is not a response architecture anymore — it is an incident disclosure process.

The unavoidable implication
A phased response

What to do,
in what order

A mature program executes across all four horizons concurrently. An organization responding specifically to this incident should start at Horizon 0 today.

Horizon 00
0–48h
Immediate
  • Rotate every credential in non-sensitive Vercel env vars
  • Flip every remaining secret to the sensitive flag
  • Search Workspace audit logs for the published IOC
  • Review Vercel activity logs for anomalous IPs and deployments
  • Enforce Standard Deployment Protection; rotate its tokens
Horizon 01
1–4w
Short-term
  • Full third-party OAuth app inventory across Workspace/M365
  • Restrict OAuth authorization to admin-approved apps for sensitive scopes
  • Enforce phishing-resistant MFA on the IdP — no SMS/TOTP fallback
  • Enable Advanced Protection Program for production-access users
  • Deploy anomaly detection on OAuth refresh events
  • Publish internal policy for vendor OAuth authorization
Horizon 02
1–3m
Medium-term
  • Migrate from static secrets to workload identity federation (OIDC)
  • Deploy device trust / posture checks for SSO into production SaaS
  • Build an OAuth tripwire routed to the SOC, not just IT
  • Automate secret rotation for remaining long-lived credentials
  • Build vendor security review program gated on SOC 2, scope justification, token-storage architecture
  • Audit your own product's OAuth surface — you are someone else's Context.ai
Horizon 03
3–12m
Long-term
  • Zero standing privilege for all non-human identities
  • Investigate Macaroons, Biscuits, SPIFFE/SPIRE, AAuth for agent-first identity
  • Continuous credential exposure monitoring against breach datasets
  • Distinct incident playbooks for third-party OAuth compromise
  • AI agent platform governance with stricter vendor review and prompt-injection threat modeling
  • Move toward proof-of-possession credentials (mTLS, DPoP, signed requests) over bearer tokens
The thesis

The Vercel incident is not technically interesting. That is what makes it important.

An attacker owned a third-party AI vendor, rode OAuth tokens into a federated identity, and read the secrets that were sitting in plaintext-readable form because the control that would have protected them defaulted to off.

The vendor that gets breached next will be different. The pattern will be identical.
